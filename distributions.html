    • Introduction

    In statistics, probability distributions may be divided into 2 categories: Discrete and Continuous. In discrete distributions, random variables take up discrete, countable values. On the other hand, in continuous distributions, random variables tend to take up a range of values. For instance, the probability distribution that models a roll on a fair dice should be a discrete distribution, since a dice has only six faces. On the other hand, the height of the human population on Earth follows a continuous distributionm, since the height variable takes up a range of real numbers.


    Discrete and continuous distributions are respectively associated with a Probability Mass Function (PMF) or Probability Density Function (PDF). For each possible value that a random variable can take (also known as the support), the PMF describes the probability of attaining that value. For example, the support of the Bernoulli is 0 or 1, since there can only be a success or a failure.


    The PDF is the continuous variant of the PMF. Since the PDF deals with a continous range of values, the PDF is itself a continuous function. As a result, interestingly, the probability of attaining a specific value is always zero. As what its name suggests, the PDF describes the "density" of probabilities at specific values, rather than describing probabilities of attaining these values. The PDF, however, describes the probability of the random variable falling within a range of values. Mathematically, this is described as the integration of the PDF over this range of values.
